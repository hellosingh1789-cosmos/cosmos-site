Cosmic Scale

MIND-BENDING FACTS

93B
Light-Year Diameter

The observable universe spans 93 billion light-years. Even travelling at the speed of light, it would take longer than the universe has existed to cross it.

2T
Galaxies Estimated

Modern telescope data suggests there are over 2 trillion galaxies in the observable universe — ten times more than previously thought.

13.8B
Years Since Big Bang

The universe began 13.8 billion years ago from an infinitely dense point. The cosmic microwave background radiation is its faint afterglow, still detectable today.

300K
km/s — Speed of Light

Light travels 299,792 km per second. Sunlight takes 8 minutes to reach Earth. The nearest star, Proxima Centauri, is 4.24 light-years away.

68%
Dark Energy

About 68% of the universe is dark energy, 27% is dark matter. Everything we can see — stars, planets, galaxies — makes up just 5% of the cosmos.

4B
Years Until Andromeda Collision

Our Milky Way is on a collision course with the Andromeda galaxy. In about 4 billion years, the two galaxies will begin merging into a single elliptical galaxy.

Human Exploration

REACHING FOR THE STARS

1957
Sputnik 1 — The Space Age Begins

The Soviet Union launches the first artificial satellite. Its radio beeps, audible on Earth, mark humanity's first foray beyond the atmosphere.

1961
Yuri Gagarin Orbits Earth

The Soviet cosmonaut becomes the first human in space, completing one orbit in 108 minutes aboard Vostok 1.

1969
Apollo 11 — First Moon Landing

Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walk on the Moon's surface. Armstrong's words — "one giant leap for mankind" — are broadcast to 600 million viewers worldwide.

1990
Hubble Space Telescope Launch

Hubble revolutionizes our understanding of the universe, capturing images of objects 13+ billion light-years away and measuring the universe's expansion rate.

2004
Mars Rovers Spirit & Opportunity

NASA's twin rovers land on Mars. Opportunity was expected to last 90 days but operated for 14 years, traveling over 45 km across the Martian surface.

2021
James Webb Space Telescope

Launched on Christmas Day, Webb can see the universe's first galaxies forming just 300 million years after the Big Bang, in unprecedented infrared detail.

The Biggest Question

ARE WE ALONE?

“

The universe is under no obligation to make sense to you.

— Neil deGrasse Tyson

There are more stars in the observable universe than grains of sand on every beach and desert on Earth. The numbers are so vast that the idea of Earth being the only world where something looks up and wonders seems not just improbable — it seems almost arrogant. Europa hides a liquid water ocean larger than all of Earth's seas combined. Enceladus jets organic molecules into space. Mars bears the scars of ancient rivers. The James Webb Space Telescope has already detected potential biosignature molecules in the atmosphere of a distant exoplanet. The scientific search for life is no longer philosophy — it is an active, funded, rapidly advancing field of science.

300million Potentially habitable planets in the Milky Way alone
5,600+ Confirmed exoplanets, with thousands more candidates
7 Earth-sized planets in TRAPPIST-1, just 39 light-years away

Then there is the silence. If the universe is so vast, so old, and so full of habitable worlds, where is everybody? The Fermi Paradox has haunted scientists since 1950, and no one has answered it convincingly. The 1977 "Wow! signal" — a 72-second narrowband radio pulse that matched every expectation of an alien transmission — was never detected again. We may be rare. We may be early. Or the cosmos may be teeming with life that simply hasn't found us yet. Right now, for the first time in the history of our species, we are actually building the tools to find out. We are just beginning to listen.
